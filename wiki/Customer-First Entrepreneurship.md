## What it is

The highest-ROI entrepreneurial move is to start with a real customer and a real pain, then validate demand via presale before building anything. Market signals willingness-to-pay before investment. The sequence matters: customer → problem → presale → build is the correct order; build → market → discover lack of demand is the common failure mode.

## Why it matters

Most failed startups built something technically impressive that nobody needed (General Magic: technically brilliant, zero customers). Reversing the sequence eliminates this failure mode at near-zero cost. Kagan argues you can validate any business idea in a weekend using this principle.

## Key points

- "Customer first, not founder first": start with access to a customer group, not a product concept (Kagan)
- Validate via presale — collect real money, not interest or compliments; compliments are free, money signals real demand
- Vitamin vs. painkiller: aim for indispensable (painkiller) not merely nice-to-have (vitamin)
- Three W's: Who are you selling to? What problem? Where do you reach them?
- The customer must recognise the problem today — not a future hypothetical (Fadell: Uber started with daily frustration, General Magic did not)
- Join revolutions, not incremental improvements (Fadell) — the market must already exist and be moving
- Execution is the differentiator (Arnold): ideas are abundant; consistent delivery over 5–7 years is rare
- Playing to win vs. playing not to lose (Gschwandtner): two fundamentally different mindsets that produce different decisions
- Build connections by sharing specific knowledge publicly before you have a product — earn respect in the domain

## Open questions

- How does "customer first" apply to nascent markets where customers don't yet know what they want? (Fadell's "join revolutions" is one answer — the revolution already exists)
- Is presale always possible? What is the minimum validation signal when a presale isn't feasible?
- Does the customer-first principle break down for deep-tech or infrastructure companies with long development cycles?

## Prompts

- Pick one idea you've been sitting on. Design a presale test you could run this weekend — who would you ask, what would you offer, and what would a "yes" look like?
- The Fadell diagnostic: does your customer recognise they have this problem today, or are you betting they'll recognise it once you build? Be honest.
