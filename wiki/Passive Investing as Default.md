## What it is

Passive index ETFs — buying all stocks in a market index at minimal cost — are the rational default strategy for retail investors. Over 10+ year periods, passive funds outperform the majority of actively managed funds because fees compound against active managers and diversification removes the need for stock-picking skill.

## Why it matters

The default alternative — savings accounts, bank-recommended funds, or individual stock selection — consistently underperforms the index after fees. The structural reason: financial advisors earn no commission from passive products, so they have an incentive to recommend more expensive alternatives.

## Key points

- Passive: buy all stocks in an index, capture market average return, minimise cost — beats most active managers over 10+ years
- MSCI World: ~1,600 companies across 23 industrialised nations; 8.7% average annual return 1975–2018 (including major crises)
- MSCI Emerging Markets: supplements MSCI World with growth markets (India, China, Brazil, Mexico, South Africa, Malaysia)
- Classic entry portfolio: 70% MSCI World + 30% MSCI Emerging Markets, or 100% MSCI ACWI (equivalent combined index)
- Total Expense Ratio (TER): 0.1–0.5%/year is the key cost lever — lower compounds significantly over decades
- Banks don't recommend ETFs because they earn no commission — a structural incentive conflict worth knowing
- Rebalance when any position deviates >20% from target allocation — otherwise do nothing
- DeMarco's counterpoint: passive investing is the "slowlane" — rational, reliable, but deliberately slow; the fastlane requires creating scalable value
