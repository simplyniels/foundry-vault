## What it is

PARA organises digital information by actionability (destination), not by topic (origin). Four buckets: Projects (active work with end date), Areas (ongoing commitments with standards, no end date), Resources (interests worth keeping, no deadline), Archive (inactive, but retrievable). The system applies identically across every app, folder, and storage layer.

## Why it matters

Most digital filing collapses under the note paradox: more notes → harder to find them → paralysis or compulsive tool-switching. PARA solves this not by finding a better category scheme but by eliminating the question "where does this belong?" and replacing it with "where will this be useful?"

## Key points

- Organise by destination (where it's going), not origin (where it came from) — the kitchen metaphor
- Projects have end dates; Areas have standards; Resources have interest; Archive is a preserve, not a trash can
- The same four buckets apply across Obsidian, email, files, cloud storage — no special-case silos
- "Move fast, touch lightly" — perfectionism in filing is wasted effort
- Progressive summarisation: original note → highlights → bold key phrases → executive summary in your own words — distillation is the core cognitive skill
- PARA turns cleanup from a never-ending activity into goal-directed work done alongside real projects
- The note paradox: more notes without PARA → slower retrieval → abandon the system → cognitive debt

## Open questions

- Does "Areas have standards, not deadlines" scale to the long-term? When does an Area become a Resource?

## Prompts

- The kitchen test: draw your current note/file structure and mark which items are organised by origin and which by destination. What would the destination-first version look like?
- Apply PARA to your Obsidian vault. Which notes belong in Projects? Which have been masquerading as Resources but are really Archives?
