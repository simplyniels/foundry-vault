**Summary**

Matthew Walker's comprehensive review of sleep science, arguing that sleep is the most powerful health intervention available — superior to diet and exercise as the foundational pillar of wellbeing. The book covers circadian biology, sleep architecture (REM and non-REM), the neuroscience of adenosine and melatonin, the cumulative costs of sleep deprivation, and practical sleep hygiene recommendations. Core claim: two thirds of adults in industrialised countries sleep fewer than the recommended eight hours, with severe health consequences.

**Key points**
- Sleep is regulated by two systems: the 24-hour circadian clock and adenosine-based sleep pressure (both must align)
- Adenosin accumulates while awake; caffeine works by blocking adenosine receptors, masking (not eliminating) the debt
- Caffeine half-life: 5–7 hours — an evening espresso still has 50% activity at midnight
- Melatonin is a timing signal, not a sleep drug; it signals darkness but doesn't produce sleep itself
- Chronotype (owl vs. lark) is largely genetic — owls forced to early schedules suffer chronic sleep deprivation
- Less than 6–7 hours per night: doubles cancer risk, accelerates Alzheimer's risk, impairs blood glucose to pre-diabetic levels
- Sleep deprivation raises hunger hormones while suppressing satiety — sleep loss causes weight gain
- Every diet attempt while sleep-deprived preferentially loses muscle, not fat
- Deep non-REM sleep begins declining in one's late twenties; by mid-forties, 60–70% of teenage deep sleep is gone
- Two diagnostic questions: could you fall back asleep by 10am? Can you function without caffeine until noon? "Yes/No" = insufficient sleep
- Sleep hygiene essentials: consistent schedule, cool dark room, no caffeine after midday, no screens before bed, no alcohol
