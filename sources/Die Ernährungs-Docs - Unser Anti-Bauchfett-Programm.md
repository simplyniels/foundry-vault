**Summary**

A nutrition medicine guide from four German physician-nutritionists (Anne Fleck, Matthias Riedl, Silja Schäfer, Jörn Klasen) on reducing visceral (belly) fat through dietary change. The core argument is that visceral fat is not passive — it is a metabolically active hormone-like tissue that drives systemic inflammation, insulin resistance, and cascading disease risk. Dietary culprits are sugar and refined carbohydrates, not fat. The prescription is Mediterranean-style eating, intermittent fasting, high protein, and targeted movement.

**Key points**
- Visceral fat acts like a hormone gland, releasing pro-inflammatory cytokines throughout the body
- Consequences include type 2 diabetes, cardiovascular disease, Alzheimer's, and certain cancers
- The culprit is sugar and fast-digesting carbohydrates, not dietary fat
- Visceral fat disrupts leptin signalling → leptinresistenz → perpetual hunger despite sufficient energy stores
- Liver: central to fat metabolism; fatty liver produces Fetuin-A, causing systemic inflammation
- Mediterranean diet outperforms low-fat diet for visceral fat reduction by one third
- Protein is the anti-belly-fat macronutrient: long satiation, activates GLP hormone, raises metabolic rate during digestion
- Intermittent fasting (16:8) accelerates fat loss without suppressing metabolism — unlike calorie restriction alone
- Good fats (olive oil, rape/canola oil, omega-3s) are anti-inflammatory and support satiation
- Stress raises cortisol → promotes belly fat; sleep deprivation compounds this
- Exercise taps belly fat preferentially because visceral tissue is metabolically active — cardio plus strength both required
- The 1600 kcal / day target: one low-carb/high-protein meal per day is the key lever
