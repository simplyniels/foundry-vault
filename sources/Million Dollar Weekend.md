**Summary**

Noah Kagan's playbook for starting a business over a weekend by validating demand before building anything. The core method is "customer first": identify who you're selling to, find a real problem they have, and presell the solution before writing a line of code or spending a dollar. The overarching philosophy is action over analysis — Kagan argues that most entrepreneurial failures stem from overthinking and under-starting, not from bad ideas.

**Key points**
- "Customer first, not founder first": start with access to a customer group, not with a product concept
- The three W's: Who are you selling to? What problem are you solving? Where do you reach them?
- The NOW-not-HOW rule: ask someone for a business idea right now rather than planning in isolation
- Selling is helping — if you believe your product improves lives, withholding it is selfish
- Validate via direct presale before building: collect real money, not interest or compliments
- Market sizing via Google Trends (demand trend) and Facebook Ads (audience size) before committing
- The 1-million-dollar test: (potential customers) × (price) ≥ $1M? Yes/No is fast enough
- Vitamin vs. painkiller: aim for products that are indispensable, not merely nice-to-have
- Flutwellen-Märkte (tidal-wave markets): ride large existing demand rather than creating new demand from scratch
- Revenue levers: order value, frequency, price, customer segment, product range, add-on services
- Content Circle Framework (Ali Abdaal case): inner niche first → expand to related audiences systematically
- The unfair advantage: what makes you uniquely credible to your inner circle?

**Claude's notes**

This is the most actionable entrepreneurship text in the vault. The presale-before-build principle is a clean inversion of the standard "build, then market" sequence. Kagan's case studies are grounded and specific. The Ali Abdaal example (starting with BMAT exam prep, building to 4.4M subscribers) appears here and directly connects to [[Feel-Good Productivity]] as a character study. The "vitamin vs. painkiller" framing is a memorable heuristic. Connects with [[Build – Ein Unorthodoxer Leitfaden]] on the imperative to solve real problems.
