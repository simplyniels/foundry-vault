**Summary**
Peter Steinberger, Austrian founder of OpenClaw (an AI agent that autonomously handles complex tasks via messaging apps like WhatsApp), accepted a ~$1 billion compensation package to join OpenAI after Sam Altman recruited him. His departure from Europe is framed as a symptom of European regulatory and cultural hostility to AI entrepreneurship: he was "screamed at" for building AI in Europe while US counterparts were enthusiastic. European AI investment ($15B in 2025) runs at 1/10th of US levels ($146B).

**Key points**
- OpenClaw: an AI agent controlling computer tasks (email, flights, bank transfers, software coding) via WhatsApp — started as a weekend project
- Steinberger previously sold PSPDFKit for $100M in 2021 after a decade of up to 100-hour weeks without outside capital
- Offered ~$1B compensation package by Sam Altman to join OpenAI; will now work from San Francisco
- European regulatory environment described as: fighting paragraphs before customers, employee participation law, paralysing labour regulations
- In Europe "Leute schreiben REGULIERUNG" (people shout REGULATION) — in the US most people are enthusiastic
- 2025 German start-up record (3,568 new companies) but large AI ideas are capitalised elsewhere
- $146B flowed into US AI start-ups in 2025 vs. ~$15B in Europe

**Claude's notes**
Steinberger is an unusually clean test case: a successful, proven founder with capital access who still left. His framing — it's not just money, it's the cultural atmosphere of enthusiasm vs. suspicion — is the sharper claim. The OpenClaw product itself (agentic AI via messaging, no GUI needed) is the consumer-facing version of what Cherny demonstrates in the developer context: AI as workforce, controllable through natural language over any channel. Europe's regulatory approach is creating a talent drain at exactly the moment when frontier AI is being built.
