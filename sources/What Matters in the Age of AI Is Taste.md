**Summary**

Argues that as AI gets better at execution, the differentiator is taste — the human ability to discern what's worth making. AI is powerful but taste-blind; it generates from the space of all possibility, and the quality of its output depends entirely on the context you give it. The practical upshot: a curated personal knowledge base is no longer just a productivity tool — it's the raw material that teaches AI to see through your eyes. Organise by context of use (not topic), and feed AI the references you've gathered.

**Key points**
- AI can make anything but doesn't know what's worth making — taste is the filter
- The quality of AI output is proportional to the quality of context you provide
- Context windows have grown from ~1,500 words to 1.5M+ words, making deep personal context feasible for the first time
- Curating beyond algorithmic feeds (archives, manifestos, personal references) produces richer, more distinctive AI output
- Organise notes by context-of-use ("references for my next essay") not by topic ("productivity") — this matches retrieval patterns and makes AI context-feeding natural
- Holly Herndon's reframe: AI as "collective intelligence" rather than "artificial intelligence" shifts the framing from replacement to collaboration
- Two worked examples (writing draft, job description) show the difference between zero-context and curated-context AI prompts
- Taste isn't a gift — it's what happens when you pay close attention to what moves you
- Gut intuition (hunches, felt patterns) deserves cultivation alongside measurable knowledge
