**Summary**

Henning Jauernig's personal-finance primer for German young adults. The central argument is that passive index investing (ETFs) is the most rational strategy for retail investors — outperforming actively managed funds over time because of lower fees and diversification. The book is a practical entry point: which indices to choose, which ETF providers exist, how to rebalance, and why banks don't recommend ETFs (they earn nothing from them).

**Key points**
- Passive index investing: capture average market return across all stocks in a market — beats most active funds over time
- MSCI World: ~1,600 companies across 23 industrialised nations; 8.7% average annual return 1975–2018 (including major crises)
- MSCI Emerging Markets: supplements MSCI World with growth markets (India, China, Brazil, Mexico, South Africa, Malaysia)
- Classic entry portfolio: 70% MSCI World + 30% MSCI Emerging Markets, or 100% MSCI ACWI (which combines both)
- Total Expense Ratio (TER) is the key cost metric: 0.1–0.5% per year; lower is better
- Major ETF providers: iShares (BlackRock), Xtrackers (Deutsche Bank), Lyxor (Société Générale), ComStage (ex-Commerzbank)
- justETF.com: recommended comparison platform for finding, comparing, and tracking ETFs
- Rebalance when a position deviates >20% from target allocation
- Banks don't recommend ETFs because they earn no commission from passive products

**Claude's notes**

A clean, practical introduction to passive investing for German young adults. The ETF thesis is well-documented and widely validated in financial literature. The specific number (8.7% MSCI World average return 1975–2018) is a useful calibration anchor. The "banks don't recommend ETFs" structural incentive point is memorable and worth using in any writing on financial literacy. This is the only personal finance source in the vault so far — single-source, will need reinforcement before a concept article on personal finance. The MSCI World / Emerging Markets portfolio is the closest thing to a consensus recommendation in passive investing literature.
